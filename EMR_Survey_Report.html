🔌 Infrastructure Takeaways

  • Hospitals dominate (58%, n=71): Eye care in these settings is predominantly hospital-based, though 32% (n=39) work in clinics and 10% in mobile, field, or multi-facility settings.
  • Mixed connectivity: 33% have reliable fast internet, but 55% face slow or intermittent connections, and 11% have very rare or time-limited access — offline-first design is non-negotiable.
  • Power instability is widespread: 44% experience daily or multiple-times-daily outages. Systems must tolerate sudden disconnection and recover gracefully without data loss.
  • 83% need offline capability: 37% say it is absolutely required; 46% say preferred but not critical. Only 15% are comfortable with always-online connectivity.

📋 EMR Landscape Takeaways

  • Paper-first reality (54%, n=66): Over half use paper only. A further 7% (n=8) have no formal system. Combined, 67% have no digital records — the starting baseline is essentially zero.
  • 27% already use an ophthalmology EMR (n=33): This group likely has the clearest awareness of existing feature gaps and will have the strongest opinions on design.
  • Ophthalmology-only preferred (61%, n=75): With 18% flexible, only 18% (n=22) strictly want a general-purpose EMR. Specialization is the dominant preference by a wide margin.
  • Wide scale range: 24% have 20+ physician users; 16% are solo practitioners. The system must scale from a single-user clinic to a large multi-doctor hospital without forcing complexity on small teams.

🎓 Training & Support Takeaways

  • Time is extremely scarce: 23% (n=28) can dedicate less than 1 hour and 34% (n=42) only 1–3 hours. 76% have 3 hours or less total — the UI must be radically self-explanatory from day one.
  • Video tutorials dominate (55%, n=68): Asynchronous, self-paced video is the clear #1 preference — respondents need to train when connectivity and time permit, not in scheduled live sessions.
  • Offline documentation is the #1 support tool (54%, n=66): When the internet is down, a built-in offline help system outranks any remote helpline or waiting strategy.
  • 24% still want a helpline (n=30): A tiered model — offline docs first, escalate to phone/chat support — would satisfy both the majority and this important minority.

27 substantive responses received (22% response rate on this optional question) · Blank, "N/A", or "No" responses excluded · Arabic responses translated to English below

📝 Thematic Summary of Open Comments

  • UI Simplicity & Speed (most frequent — ~8 mentions): Respondents independently emphasized a clean, uncluttered, fast interface. Laggy systems in high-volume clinics are described as unacceptable and disruptive.
  • Network & Power Resilience (~5 mentions): Providers in Pakistan and Syria repeatedly cited unreliable power and internet as the core daily challenge, and asked the system to work reliably below 10 Mbps.
  • Customisability Without a Developer (~4 mentions): Multiple Pakistani respondents independently asked to add or modify fields without needing a software engineer — a no-code configuration layer is the most consistently repeated technical request.
  • Referral & Follow-up Tracking (2 mentions): Providers sending patients to larger hospitals for surgery need to track post-referral outcomes — currently a major operational blind spot.
  • Direct Prescription Printing (1 mention): Printing prescriptions directly from the system is highlighted as a significant time-saver versus current hand-writing.
  • Patient Photo ID (1 mention): Serving patients without official ID, respondents want to attach a photo to patient profiles for visual recognition on return visits.
  • AI-Assisted Dictation (1 Arabic mention): Detailed manual data entry is too slow in high-volume settings — AI voice-to-text with human review capability was explicitly and specifically requested.
  • Role-Based Data Entry (1 Arabic mention): Nursing staff should handle data input to free up physician time — role-based workflows and permissions matter.

Conclusions
Strategic Design Recommendations
🔌 Offline-First Architecture

Build with offline as the primary mode, not a fallback. Local data must be fully functional without internet. Cloud sync must happen opportunistically in the background when connectivity is restored, without requiring user action or risking data loss during power cuts.

🔬 Medical Imaging as Core

88% require image storage — it is not optional. Efficient compression, local storage, and fast retrieval on low-spec devices must be designed in from day one. Eye scan storage and photo-linking to patient records are table-stakes features.

🛡️ Flexible Patient Identity

A significant share of patients lack official ID. The system must support: attached patient photo, name + date of birth + physical description, and locally-generated ID numbers — all without requiring a national ID card or passport.

👁️ Ophthalmology-Specialised UI

Design primarily for ophthalmic workflows: visual acuity, auto-refraction, lens prescriptions, surgical follow-up, cataract tracking. General medicine should be a clean optional extension, not the default screen layout or data model.

🌍 Arabic & English RTL Support

35% of respondents are Arabic-speaking. Full RTL Arabic layout is required — not just translated strings. Interface direction, input behavior, numeric formatting, and document printing must all support right-to-left natively.

⚙️ No-Code Customisation

Multiple respondents independently requested the ability to add fields and features without a software engineer. A no-code configuration layer for custom fields, forms, and templates — manageable by clinic staff — directly addresses this recurring need.

🔁 Referral & Follow-up Tracking

Providers routinely lose track of patients referred to larger facilities. A lightweight referral module recording destination, date, procedure, and post-referral outcome would close a real clinical gap — especially critical for cataract surgery follow-up.

🖥️ Radical UI Simplicity

With limited digital literacy, ≤3 hours for training (76% of respondents), and high patient volumes, every extra click compounds. The interface must be immediately intuitive. Prescription printing from within the system should be a single-click action.

🤖 AI-Assisted Data Entry

Respondents from Syria explicitly requested AI-assisted dictation with human review capability. In high-volume settings where detailed typing is impractical, voice-to-text or smart form auto-fill could dramatically reduce the documentation burden on physicians.
